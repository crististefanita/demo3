## What The Library Must Teach

- the application owns the graph; the package is only the overlay;
- `root diff` and `run diff` are the primary truth of evolution;
- Extent/XML, state files, gate card, render proof, and link audit are mandatory;
- `Score Overview` is computed cold, not decorated;
- `tests produced` and `runs consumed` do not mix;
- `Overall` stays capped by business substance;
- good reporting does not turn a positive shell into a persistent object;
- the top of the page should stay calm, short, and reusable.

## What The Library Must Not Teach

- reporting sold as product value;
- a test-suite graph instead of an application graph;
- missing `run diff`;
- missing `package-state.json`, `run-state.json`, `execution-gate-card.md`;
- a final HTML page that talks about `html-ex`, `teacher`, or `benchmark`;
- inflated `Overall` above the real business object;
- too much procedural text inside graph nodes;
- raw artifacts treated as the owner of truth.

## Cold Calibration Formula

The library is good when it:

- teaches you to reopen the package cold;
- forces you to name the real business object;
- stops you from over-scoring;
- shows clearly where the product broke.

The library is used badly when you:

- choose an example only because it looks good;
- copy its business or its verdict;
- use pretty graphs as a substitute for real persistent objects;
- raise the score without `order row`, `invoice row`, `ticket id`, `case row`, `message row`, or another comparable object.
